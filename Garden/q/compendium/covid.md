What does the "19" in "COVID-19" refer to?
1. There are 19 variants of the coronavirus.
2. There are 19 symptoms of coronavirus disease.
3. This is the 19th coronavirus pandemic.
4. The coronavirus and the disease it causes were identified in 2019.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>4. The coronavirus and the disease it causes were identified in 2019. A new coronavirus, technically named SARS-CoV-2, and the disease it causes were first identified in 2019.

True or false: There is more than one coronavirus.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>1. True. There are hundreds of coronaviruses, and some of them cause disease in humans, such as mild colds and more serious illnesses or death. The SARS-CoV-2 coronavirus, along with those that cause SARS and MERS are examples of coronaviruses that can cause serious illness in people.

The "corona" in coronavirus means:
1. Sun.
2. Beer.
3. Strong.
4. Crown.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>4. Crown. Spike proteins on these viruses' outer coating give them the appearance of a crown or halo.

True or false: In other countries outside the Philippines, once you are fully vaccinated you may not be required to wear a mask in public places.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>True. The CDC has updated its recommendations on resuming some activities once you have completed your COVID-19 vaccines and allowed at least two weeks for your immune system to respond after your last shot. Fully vaccinated people can resume activities without wearing a mask or physically distancing, except where required by federal, state, local, tribal, or territorial laws, rules, and regulations, including local business and workplace guidance. The CDC continues to recommend that masks and physical distancing are required when going to the doctor's office, hospitals or long-term care facilities, including all Johns Hopkins hospitals, care centers and offices.

When is it safe to be closer than 6 feet to another person who is not sick with COVID-19?
1. When the person is related to you.
2. When you have already had COVID-19.
3. When the person normally lives with you.
4. When you are outdoors.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>3. When the person normally lives with you. When you're around anyone from outside your regular household, physical distancing and mask-wearing are essential to avoid spreading the coronavirus. Outdoors is generally safer than inside, but you should still maintain physical distance and wear your face mask.

Staying apart from other people when you have been exposed to the coronavirus is called:
1. Physical distancing
2. Isolation
3. Quarantine
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>3. Quarantine. A person who has been exposed to an infectious illness might be under quarantine to wait and see if they will become sick. Physical distancing refers to staying at least 6 feet apart from someone to avoid spreading disease. Isolation is keeping someone sick with COVID-19 apart from other patients in a health care setting.

What is the safest way to celebrate your birthday during the COVID-19 pandemic?
1. Sharing a meal with relatives only.
2. Having a virtual party using an online conferencing app.
3. Hitting your favorite bar when it's less crowded.
4. Gathering at a friend's house with people you know well.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>2. Having a virtual party using an online conferencing app. Having a virtual party is the safest option. Gathering in person, especially indoors, with people that you don't normally live with — even if they're relatives or good friends — is risky. Situations where masks aren't being worn or are being removed to eat or drink are especially likely to spread the coronavirus and cause illness.

True or false: Having a food allergy means you should not get a COVID-19 vaccine.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>False. People with food allergies can be vaccinated for the coronavirus. However, those who are seriously allergic to any of the ingredients in the vaccine itself should not get the vaccine.

3. Severe COVID-19 illness.
4. Equivocal COVID-19 illness.

>2. This patient has moderate COVID-19 illness. The National Institutes of Health groups adults with severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) infection (the coronavirus that causes COVID-19) into the following severity of illness categories.' However, the criteria for each category may overlap or vary across clinical guidelines and clinical trials, and a patient's clinical status may change over time.

Further evaluation of Ms. S reveals a normal chest x-ray. Her initial battery of tests reveals a normal blood count and normal chemistries except a random glucose of 175 mg/dL, hemoglobin AlC of 8%, and a mildly elevated ferritin and D-dimer. Ms. Slives at home with her husband, who is well. They have reliable transportation, live 2 miles from a hospital, and have dependable cell service. What would be the most appropriate treatment to discuss with Ms. S for her or her husband?
1. Administer vitamin D and vitamin super B complex as postexposure prophylaxis for her husband.
2. Administer bamlanivimab for Ms. S.
3. Administer dexamethasone for Ms. S.
4. Administer remdesivir for Ms. S.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>2. Bamlanivimab is a neutralizing monoclonal antibody that targets the receptor-binding domain of the spike protein of SARS-CoV-2. An interim analysis of the BLAZE-1 study, a phase 2, randomized, placebo-controlled trial, suggested a potential clinical benefit of bamlanivimab for outpatients with mild to moderate COVID-19, with up to a 10% absolute decrease in emergency department or hospital admissions when given to high-risk groups. Because of her age and comorbidities, Ms. S is considered a high-risk patient. On November 9, 2020, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued an emergency use authorization (EUA) to make bamlanivimab available for the treatment of nonhospitalized patients with mild-to-moderate COVID-19 at high risk for progressing to severe disease and/or hospitalization. ' However, the COVID-19 Treatment Guidelines Panel (the Panel) notes that bamlanivimab should not be considered the standard of care for the treatment of patients with mild-to-moderate COVID-19 not requiring hospitalization until sufficient data demonstrate benefit. The Panel recommends against the use of any agents for SARS-CoV-2 preexposure or postexposure prophylaxis, except in clinical trials. Dexamethasone and remdesivir are therapeutic options only for patients with COVID-19 who are hospitalized and require supplemental oxygen.

Ms. S's contact tracing reveals the following close contacts: a daughter who had a well-documented case of COVID-19 2 months ago and who lives alone; 2 sons living with their families; 2 single friends living alone; and Ms. S's husband. They are unsure about who should quarantine and who should be tested. Which of the following statements is true?
1. Ms. S's daughter should quarantine for 14 days because of the known risk of reinfection.
2. Quarantine options are determined by local medical providers.
3. Ms. S's sons should quarantine for 7 days, but a test is not needed if they have no symptoms.
4. Ms. S's husband should be tested for COVID-19, and if positive, he can isolate with Ms. S.
>[!FAQ]- Answer
>4. In this situation, like so many, it is challenging to find explicit clinical practice recommendations because each situation is unique. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends that all people quarantine if they have been in close contact with someone who has COVID-19-excluding people who have had COVID-19 symptoms within the past 3 months. The CDC defines a "close contact" as someone who was within 6 feet of an infected person for a cumulative total of 15 minutes or more over a 24-hour period starting from 2 days before illness onset (or, for asymptomatic patients, 2 days prior to test specimen collection) until the time the patient is isolated.
